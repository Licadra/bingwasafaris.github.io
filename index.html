Meet the Big Five
of Meru

Meru National Park is brilliant on a magnificent scale, and it featurea luxuriant jungle, coursing rivers, verdant swamp, khaki grasslands and a variety of wild animals all under the sky’s great blue bowl. Very few places are comparable to the remote and rugged atmosphere found here. Visitors can see the big five: elephant, rhino, lion, buffalo and leopard along with Grevy's zebras, Bohor reedbucks, hartebeests, pythons, puff adders, cobras, buffalos and more than 400 recorded species of birds.

Meru is, of course, the land of the Big Five – elephant, rhino, lion, buffalo, and leopard.

Meru National park is home to 34 block rhinos and 70 white rhinos.

The park also homes some of the largest herds of buffaloes.

Get to see the strongest of Africa’s cats by far, the mighty Lion. They never fail to produce a memorable sighting.

Nyambene Forest and

Water Tower

The excursion takes visitors through scenic landscapes
where some of the best quality tea in the world is grown.
It culminates in the visit to Nyambene forest and water tower
and includes a tour of the sources and waterworks of the Tuuru Water Scheme,
an endevour of the Catholic Diocese of Meru and the local communities.
The Tuuru Water Scheme distributes water over an area of 1,200 sq. KM
of both low and high population densities.
The aqueducts extend over 250 km and channel over 36 million
liters of water per day to more than a quarter million people and
more than 60,000 large and small domestic animals.

Half-day and full-day options may include packed lunch in Nyambene Forest (by arrangement).

Magado [Igombe] Crater –

Nyambene National Reserve

This excursion takes visitor to the Magado Crater
[also known as Igombe among the Meru] located
within the Nyambene National Reserve.
The crater is a spectacular site with dark purple, bright red and green pools.
A lake on the floor of the crater evaporates to form soda salt crust.
Meru people have been collecting soda in this crater since time immemorial.
More recently, Borana and Somali people have been sharing the crater.
The local people water their cattle in this natural collecting basin.
During the dry season as the full lake recedes, the soda that remains behind is collected.

The cost covers for a ranger to accompany visitors.
